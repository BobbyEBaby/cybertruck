### Hook — TTS narration (0:13–0:33, ~52 words)

In July of 1518, in the city of Strasbourg, a woman stepped out into the street and began to dance. <!-- Waller 2009, Lancet; Nat Geo; Public Domain Review -->
She did not stop. Not that day. Not that night. Not the next morning.
Within a week, more than thirty people had joined her. Within a month, the number was in the hundreds. <!-- Wikipedia; History.com; Smithsonian -->
And people were beginning to die.

---

### Context — TTS narration (0:33–1:42, ~175 words)

Strasbourg, in 1518, was a walled city in the Holy Roman Empire, in the region we now call Alsace. It was prosperous on paper and desperate underneath. <!-- Britannica; Nat Geo -->

Three years of failed harvests had pushed grain prices beyond what ordinary families could pay. Smallpox and syphilis were moving through the poorer quarters. A peasant revolt had been crushed a few years earlier, and the people who survived it were still hungry. <!-- Nat Geo; National Geographic; History.com -->

The Reformation was one year away. Martin Luther would nail his theses to a church door in Wittenberg the autumn after the plague ended, but the religious anxiety that fed that moment was already in the air. People genuinely believed that saints could punish you. Specifically, they believed that a saint named Vitus could strike you with an uncontrollable, deadly dance — if you angered him, or if God permitted it as a test. <!-- Waller 2009, Lancet; Public Domain Review -->

So when a woman started dancing alone in the street and would not stop, the people of Strasbourg already had a word for what they were looking at. They called it the dance of Saint Vitus.

---

### Story, beat one — Frau Troffea — TTS narration (1:42–3:18, ~212 words)

Her name, in the city records, is given as Frau Troffea. Some sources write it as Trauffea. We don't know much more about her — not her age, not her occupation, not whether she had children at home. What the chronicles do say is that on or around the fourteenth of July, she walked into the street outside her house and started to move. <!-- Wikipedia; Public Domain Review; Smithsonian -->

Not gracefully. Not in rhythm to any music. The accounts describe convulsive, spasmodic motion — her arms jerking, her feet stamping, her body refusing to stop. Her husband came out and begged her to stop. She didn't. Or couldn't. <!-- Smithsonian; Public Domain Review -->

By nightfall she had collapsed from exhaustion. By morning she was dancing again, on feet that were now swollen and bloody.

Within a few days, other women had joined her. Then men. The chronicles — physician's notes, sermons, and minutes of the Strasbourg city council — describe a crowd gathering first in disbelief, then in panic, then in participation. By the end of the first week, roughly thirty people were dancing with her. By the end of the first month, contemporary accounts put the number at around four hundred. <!-- Wikipedia; History.com; Waller 2009, Lancet -->

And the authorities of Strasbourg had to decide what to do.

### `[HUMAN_RECORD]` — editorial commentary beat one (Robert, ~18 sec, ~45 words)

What gets me here is that nobody in this story is stupid. They're frightened, they're hungry, they're watching a woman they know break down in public — and the interpretation available to them, that a saint is doing this, is the serious, responsible, grown-up interpretation.

### Story, beat two — the authorities' response — TTS narration (3:36–5:02, ~210 words)

Here is the part that is almost impossible to believe, and that is documented in the city council's own minutes. <!-- Waller 2008; Public Domain Review; Nat Geo -->

The council brought in physicians. The physicians ruled out sin, ruled out possession, and landed on a diagnosis they called "hot blood." Their prescribed cure was to let the sufferers dance it out of their system. So the council took them at their word.

They cleared two guild halls and part of the grain market. They built a wooden stage. They hired professional musicians — pipers, drummers, horn players — and paid them to provide music around the clock. They even hired strong young men to hold the dancers upright when they began to stumble, so that the dancing would not be interrupted. <!-- Waller 2008; Public Domain Review -->

The logic was coherent inside their worldview. If the affliction was a humoral imbalance that had to be discharged through motion, then more motion, faster, was the treatment.

What actually happened is that the crowd grew. People who had been watching joined in. The dancing went on for days and weeks in the July heat. And the dancers began to collapse — from heart attacks, from strokes, from sheer exhaustion. <!-- Nat Geo; History.com; Britannica -->

The cure was killing people faster than the disease.

---

### `[HUMAN_RECORD]` — editorial commentary beat two (Robert, ~18 sec, ~45 words)

The thing I keep coming back to is that the council wasn't cruel. They hired musicians. They hired the guys to hold people up. They spent real money on this. The failure here isn't callousness — it's a coherent theory of the world, applied with full commitment, that was wrong.

---

### Story, beat three — how it ended — TTS narration (5:20–6:32, ~175 words)

When the bodies started accumulating, the council reversed itself. They banned music in public. They banned dancing in the streets. <!-- Public Domain Review; Wikipedia -->

Then, grasping at the theological interpretation they had rejected a few weeks earlier, they loaded the surviving dancers into carts and sent them on a pilgrimage — a day's walk into the hills above Saverne, to a shrine of Saint Vitus. <!-- Waller 2008; Nat Geo; History.com -->

There, priests placed small red shoes painted with crosses on the dancers' feet. They sprinkled holy water. They burned incense. They chanted over each person in turn. <!-- Public Domain Review; Crazy Alchemist; Britannica -->

And then, gradually, across August and into September, the dancing stopped. <!-- Britannica; Wikipedia -->

The contemporary Strasbourg chronicles do not record a final death toll. A later chronicle claims fifteen people a day were dying at the peak, which historians now treat as probably exaggerated. The most honest answer is that dozens died — that the dancing started in mid-July, ended in early September, and killed an unknown number of people whose names are not in any record. <!-- Wikipedia; Smithsonian; Waller 2009, Lancet -->

I want to be careful here, because it's easy to tell this story and then explain it. I don't want to explain it. I think the honest version is: something happened in Strasbourg that we still don't fully understand, and the people who were there didn't either.

---

### Why it matters — TTS narration (6:50–7:50, ~158 words)

There are three serious theories for what happened in Strasbourg, and historians still argue about them. The first is ergot poisoning — a fungus on rye that can cause convulsions and hallucinations. The medical historian John Waller, who has written the most careful book on this, rejects it, because ergot typically causes gangrene that the chronicles don't describe, and because rye wasn't the staple grain in every town where dancing epidemics occurred. <!-- Waller 2008; Waller 2009, Lancet; Nat Geo -->

The second is mass psychogenic illness — real physical symptoms produced by extreme collective stress in a population that already believed a specific saint could cause a specific kind of involuntary dance. This is Waller's preferred reading. <!-- Waller 2009, Lancet; Nat Geo -->

The third is a form of religious trance state, triggered and sustained by the ritual environment itself. <!-- Public Domain Review; Dance Research 2017 -->

None of these theories is proven. All of them are taken seriously by people who have read the sources.

If you want to read further, the book I trust most on this is John Waller's "A Time to Dance, a Time to Die" from 2008. His article in The Lancet from 2009 is shorter and free to read.
