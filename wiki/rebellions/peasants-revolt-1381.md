# Peasants' Revolt of 1381

A mass uprising in southeast England in June 1381, triggered by the third in a series of poll taxes imposed to finance a failing war, rooted in three decades of economic pressure from the labour-shortage aftermath of the [[../concepts/black-death]], and crushed within days of its high point at the [[#Smithfield parley|Smithfield parley]] on June 15. Despite being defeated tactically and having all of its stated concessions revoked within weeks, the structural conditions that produced it — and the specific legal institution of [[../concepts/villeinage]] — were effectively dead in England within a century. The canonical illustration of [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]].

## Causes — the conditions that had to be in place

The [[../concepts/black-death]] (1348–1351) killed somewhere between a third and a half of the English population. Three decades on, the surviving generation's labour was worth more than it had ever been worth before. Wages should have risen. In many places they did.

The English Parliament panicked. In 1351 it passed the [[../concepts/statute-of-labourers]], freezing wages at pre-plague levels and forbidding peasants from leaving one manor to seek better pay at another. It was, in effect, an attempt to legislate the Black Death's economic consequences out of existence.

Meanwhile, the Hundred Years' War with France had been grinding on since 1337 and by 1381 was in its fourth decade and going badly. England's treasury was empty. To fund the war, Parliament passed three successive poll taxes in 1377, 1379, and 1381. The 1381 tax — 12 pence per head, flat — was the cruelest. Rumors spread of collectors harassing women to verify their status. In Essex, a tax collector was chased off. In Kent, a court officer was killed. Within a week, the rebellion was spreading across southeast England faster than news could carry.

## The events

### The march on London (June 12–13, 1381)

Tens of thousands of peasants from Kent and Essex converged on London. [[../figures/wat-tyler]] emerged at the front. Sympathizers inside the city opened the gates. The rebels burned the Savoy Palace — the residence of [[../figures/john-of-gaunt]], the uncle they blamed for everything — to the ground. According to the chroniclers, they smashed every gold and silver object rather than looting anything, because they wanted the world to know they were rebels and not thieves. They stormed the Temple and burned the legal records. They dragged [[../figures/simon-sudbury]] (Archbishop of Canterbury and Lord Chancellor) and Robert Hales (Lord Treasurer) out of the Tower of London and beheaded them on Tower Hill. Their heads went up on London Bridge.

### The Smithfield parley (June 15, 1381)

[[../figures/richard-ii]], aged 14, genuinely trapped and with no army in the city, sent word that he would meet the rebels at Smithfield, the open ground just outside the city walls.

[[../figures/wat-tyler]] rode forward on a small horse to parley. He dismounted and bowed to the king, then — by every chronicler's account — behaved with what they called *insolent familiarity*: he asked for a drink, rinsed his mouth rudely, and demanded the end of [[../concepts/villeinage]], the end of forest law, a pardon for every rebel, and the execution of every "traitor" against the crown.

The king, astonishingly, agreed to all of it.

What happened next is disputed. Some chroniclers say Tyler insulted one of the king's retinue. Some say he reached for his dagger. [[../figures/william-walworth]], the Lord Mayor of London, rode forward and stabbed Wat Tyler in the neck. Tyler collapsed. The crowd — tens of thousands of them — drew their longbows.

And [[../figures/richard-ii]], 14 years old, rode his horse forward alone toward the arrows. According to the chronicler Thomas Walsingham, he said: *"Sirs, will you shoot your king? I will be your captain. You shall have from me that which you seek."*

The crowd, stunned, lowered their bows, and followed him out of Smithfield. Within hours, the London militia was mobilized. Within days, the rebel leaders were being hunted down across the southeast. Within weeks, every promise Richard had made was formally revoked. A royal judge told a captured group of villeins: *"Villeins you were, and villeins you are. In bondage you shall remain, not as you have hitherto done, but incomparably harsher."*

## Why it matters

On the surface, the rebellion was a total failure. The revolt was crushed. The leaders were killed. The promises were withdrawn. The labour laws the rebels died to overthrow were reaffirmed, more harshly than before.

And yet — within a hundred years, [[../concepts/villeinage]] was effectively dead in England. Not because the crown changed its mind. Because the peasants, quietly, individually, moved anyway. Wages rose anyway. Lords who tried to hold their serfs found they could not compete with lords who paid for workers. The [[../concepts/statute-of-labourers]] became unenforceable, not because it was repealed — it wasn't, not for a long time — but because the underlying economic pressure did not go away just because the rebellion did.
