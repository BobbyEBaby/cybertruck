# Simon Sudbury

Archbishop of Canterbury and Lord Chancellor of England. Murdered by the rebels of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] on Tower Hill, London, on June 14, 1381 — the day before the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381#The Smithfield parley June 15 1381|Smithfield parley]].

## Role in the Peasants' Revolt

- As Lord Chancellor, Sudbury was one of the principal architects of the [[../concepts/poll-tax-1381]]
- The rebels therefore held him directly responsible for the tax that triggered the uprising
- He was sheltering in the Tower of London when the rebels stormed it on June 14
- The rebels dragged him out of the Tower, along with Robert Hales (Lord Treasurer), and took them to Tower Hill
- Both men were beheaded by the crowd. Sudbury's head was mounted on London Bridge alongside other rebel trophies, where his mitre was attached by a nail to emphasize his ecclesiastical rank
- His skull is preserved to this day at St. Gregory's Church in Sudbury, Suffolk, his birthplace

## Significance

Sudbury is the purest example of the [[../themes/government]] theme in the Peasants' Revolt. The rebels did not kill [[richard-ii]] (the crown), and they could not reach [[john-of-gaunt]] (the de facto ruler, who was on campaign in Scotland). They killed the man whose name was on the tax. To the rebels, he was the government itself, in a single body.

The brutality of his execution — a full public beheading of the second-most-senior cleric in England — is a signal of how far the state's legitimacy had collapsed. To kill an archbishop in 1381 was to commit an act so theologically fraught that it could only happen in a moment of total institutional failure. Which is exactly what 1381 was.
