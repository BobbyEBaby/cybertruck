# John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry (1859)

A thirty-six-hour armed seizure of the United States federal arsenal at Harpers Ferry, Virginia, by [[figures/john-brown]] and twenty-one followers — sixteen white, five Black — on October 16–18, 1859. Tactically a complete failure: the anticipated general slave uprising never materialized, ten of the raiders were killed, Brown was wounded, captured, tried for treason, and hanged on December 2, 1859.

Structurally, it may be the most consequential failed rebellion in American history. It hardened every Southern state into believing that Northern abolitionism had graduated from rhetoric to armed invasion, and it hardened every Northern abolitionist into believing that peaceful politics on the slavery question had exhausted itself. South Carolina seceded within eighteen months. Four years later slavery ended.

## Principal figures

- [[figures/john-brown]] — leader, Calvinist abolitionist, 59 at the time of the raid
- [[figures/robert-e-lee]] — at the time a U.S. Army colonel, commanded the Marine force that captured Brown in the engine house. Two years later he would command the Confederate Army of Northern Virginia.

## Key concepts

Harpers Ferry scores the lowest total of any episode so far (8/18 versus 14 for 1381 and 12 for 1525), yet its structural impact was among the largest of any rebellion in the database. This is the second time the channel's thesis has been stress-tested. The first was the German Peasants' War of 1525, where [[concepts/second-serfdom]] showed that the structural outcome can run the *opposite* direction from the thesis. Harpers Ferry is a different stress: not "the thesis was wrong," but "the framework doesn't see what actually ignited this one."

## Outcome

`triggered_reform` — the raid itself was crushed, but it precipitated the political collapse that led to the Civil War (1861–65) and the Thirteenth Amendment (1865). Six years from Brown's hanging to the end of legal slavery in the United States.
