# Episode 2 — When The Thesis Breaks: The German Peasants' War of 1524

## Script

### Introduction

Last episode, I told you that the conditions causing a rebellion usually win over the century that follows, even when the rebellion itself is crushed. Twenty thousand medieval peasants storm London, their leaders are killed within a day, the crown revokes every promise — and a hundred years later, serfdom in England is quietly dead anyway. Tactical loss, structural win.

That was a clean story. Today's episode is not.

I'm your narrator. This is Breaking Points, episode two, and today we are going to Germany, 1524. The German Peasants' War — the largest popular uprising anywhere in Europe before the French Revolution almost three hundred years later. Up to three hundred thousand people in arms. Up to a hundred thousand of them killed. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War --> Martin Luther publicly endorsed them, and then publicly called for them to be slaughtered like mad dogs, in writing, within a single year.

And here's why this episode matters for the channel. The thesis I gave you last week — that the structural conditions always win, eventually — is the kind of claim you should not trust until you've watched someone honestly try to break it. So today, I am going to try to break it with this rebellion. Because the honest answer for 1525 is messier than 1381, and the viewer deserves the mess.

Same six themes, same zero-to-three scale. Let's go.

### Body — The story

The year is 1524. The place is the Holy Roman Empire — a patchwork of several hundred territories, free cities, prince-bishoprics, and knightly lordships, nominally under Emperor Charles the Fifth, who is at this moment not in Germany at all but in Spain, trying to fight France in Italy. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War -->

That absence matters. There's our first theme: royalty. But it plays differently than 1381. The king is not a boy. The king is not in the room. The king is a continent away, and the peasants are not really angry with him. They are angry with the hundreds of local lords and prince-bishops and abbots who have, across the previous generation, been quietly tightening the screws.

Because — and this is where it gets interesting — across the decades before 1524, feudal dues in much of southern Germany had been going up, not down. New silver from the mines of Tyrol and Bohemia was flooding Europe, prices were rising, and lords were passing the cost onto peasants. Old grazing rights on common land were being enclosed for hunting reserves. Personal serfdom, which had been quietly fading in parts of Swabia, was being reintroduced on paper. Tithes — the one-tenth of every harvest owed to the Church — were being collected more aggressively, and often sold to absentee landlords who had bought the right to them as a financial asset. <!-- source: https://www.history.ox.ac.uk/article/the-german-peasants-war-15241525 -->

There are our themes — economy, government, and debt — arriving all at once and all at maximum intensity. This is not, like 1381, a single poll tax that tipped an otherwise stable system over the edge. This is a generation of compounding extraction.

Then, across this tinder, falls Martin Luther.

In 1517, Luther nails his Ninety-Five Theses to a church door in Wittenberg. By 1522, his German Bible is in print. Ordinary peasants — many of them newly literate, or listening to someone else read aloud — are reading Scripture themselves for the first time, and noticing that the New Testament does not mention tithes to an absentee bishop. Luther is not telling them to rebel. He is telling them that no human authority stands between them and God. You can see how those two ideas might combine in a mind that is already hungry.

The rising begins in the summer of 1524 in the Black Forest, spreads across Swabia through that winter, and by March of 1525 is burning across most of southern and central Germany. The numbers are astonishing. Peasant armies of five, ten, twenty thousand. Monasteries sacked. Towns joining. Whole regions out of the princes' control.

And in late February and early March 1525, in the free city of Memmingen, a furrier named Sebastian Lotzer and a preacher named Christoph Schappeler take more than three hundred separate grievance lists from peasant bands across Upper Swabia and distill them into a single document: the Twelve Articles of the Swabian Peasants. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Articles -->

The Twelve Articles are worth remembering, because they are polite. The first article asks for the right to elect their own pastor. The second article says the great tithe — the grain tithe — is fine, but it should go to the community pastor, not sold off to an absentee financier, and the little tithe, on livestock, should simply be abolished because God did not create cattle for man to owe other men a tenth of them. Other articles ask for the right to hunt and fish, an end to the reintroduced personal serfdom, the restoration of common lands, fair rents, fair justice. Every article cites Scripture.

Printed in Augsburg, the Twelve Articles are reprinted at least twenty-five times in two months — roughly twenty-five thousand copies into a world where most books sold a few hundred. It is, by some measures, the first mass media document of the Reformation era that was not written by Luther himself. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Articles -->

So Luther reads them. And Luther is caught.

In April 1525, he writes an Admonition to Peace. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Against_the_Murderous,_Thieving_Hordes_of_Peasants --> In it, he tells the princes they are tyrants whose greed brought this on themselves, and he tells the peasants that most of their twelve articles are just and biblical but they must not rise in arms because the sword belongs only to the magistrate. It is a measured piece of writing. It pleases almost no one.

And then, in May 1525, with news arriving of peasant armies burning monasteries and a radical preacher named Thomas Müntzer leading an army of around eight thousand in Thuringia under a banner of the rainbow, Luther writes a second pamphlet. The printers retitle it, without his consent, Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Against_the_Murderous,_Thieving_Hordes_of_Peasants --> In it, he tells the German princes that a rebel against authority is like a mad dog that must be struck down — *"stab, smite, slay, whoever can. If you die in doing it, well for you; a more blessed death you could never have."*

On May 14 and 15, 1525, at Frankenhausen in Thuringia, the peasant army under Müntzer is destroyed by the professional troops of Landgrave Philip of Hesse and Duke George of Saxony. Seven thousand peasants killed, by most estimates. Landsknecht losses are reported as six. It is not a battle. It is a massacre. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Frankenhausen --> Müntzer is captured, tortured for almost two weeks, and beheaded at Mühlhausen on May 27.

Across that summer and the following two years, every major peasant army is hunted down and broken. Reprisals run for two years after the fighting formally stops. The final death toll — peasants, civilians, soldiers — is around one hundred thousand. Out of perhaps three hundred thousand who rose. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War -->

### Conclusion

So. Here is where the channel's thesis meets its first real test.

Last week, I told you rebellions lose tactically and win structurally. 1381 was a clean illustration: serfdom was reaffirmed on paper, and quietly dead within a hundred years. So what about 1525?

The honest answer is: it is complicated, and mostly it went the other way.

In southern Germany — roughly, the regions where the rebellion had been thickest — serfdom did slowly weaken over the following centuries, and the specific financial form of the great tithe became harder for absentee landlords to defend. A weak, partial structural win. But in East Prussia and the territories east of the Elbe, the princes went the other direction entirely: they introduced what historians call *the second serfdom*, tightening peasant obligations over the following century. <!-- source: https://jacobin.com/2023/12/german-peasants-war-feudalism-class-conflict-reformation --> The war of 1525, in the east, did not weaken feudalism. It cemented it for another three hundred years.

And the Reformation, which Luther had launched, ends up bound to princely power in order to survive — which is another way of saying that the peasants lost their natural ally, because Luther and his successors needed the princes more than the princes needed them. <!-- source: https://witness.lcms.org/2025/when-the-lord-has-not-spoken-what-the-german-peasants-war-teaches-about-political-life-today/ -->

For 1525, the intensity rating across our six themes comes out to twelve out of eighteen. Lower than 1381's fourteen — and the reason is worth noticing. Royalty is low, because Charles the Fifth was not in the room. Environment is low, because this is not a plague story. Wars is background, not cause. Economy, government, and debt are all at maximum — this is a rebellion of structural extraction, not of sudden shock.

And yet the outcome was worse. More people died. More peasants ended the century under harder obligations than when it began. The thesis — that the pressure always has to go somewhere and that somewhere tends to be toward the rebellion's stated goals — doesn't cleanly survive 1525.

Here is what I want you to take away, honestly. The channel's thesis is not a law. It is a tendency. Some rebellions really are crushed both tactically and structurally. Some conditions really do get worse, for a long time, because a rising failed. Honest history has to hold that possibility as a live one, not explain it away. If I had not told you this, the channel would be doing the thing I promised at the start of episode one we would never do — making the pattern look cleaner than the record.

So here is the updated version of the thesis, two episodes in. *Rebellions usually lose tactically, and the conditions that caused them usually — but not always — win structurally over the following century.* The "usually" is doing real work now, and it will keep doing real work across every future episode.
