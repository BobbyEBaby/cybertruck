# Theme — Debt

One of the six recurring conditions. Debt covers **sovereign debt, personal debt, and religious debt (indulgences, tithes, forced donations) — any claim that demands payment from someone who feels the claim is illegitimate**.

## How to rate intensity (0–3)

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **0** | No significant debt dimension |
| **1** | Some burden of debt exists but isn't a rallying grievance |
| **2** | A specific debt (e.g. a tax to service a war loan) is a named grievance |
| **3** | The rebellion is *about* cancelling or repudiating debt (jubilees, tax strikes, debt forgiveness demands) |

## What to look for

- **The tax that services a loan.** When the government borrows money to fight a war and then passes the repayment to the population as taxation, that is debt dressed as taxation. See [[../concepts/poll-tax-1381]].
- **Flat debts.** A debt that falls on rich and poor alike at the same nominal amount is felt as robbery by the poor and a rounding error by the rich. Flat taxes are the classic example.
- **Religious debt.** Tithes to the church, compulsory indulgences, forced donations to religious orders. Usually combines with [[government]] theme because the church functions as an administrative apparatus.

## Observations across rebellions

### [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] — intensity **2/3**
The [[../concepts/poll-tax-1381]] was war debt extracted from the peasantry. England's treasury was empty after forty years of failed war with France. The crown borrowed to fight and then collected from the poor to repay. The rebels saw this clearly — they understood they were being taxed not for a war they chose but for a war they had lost. But the rebellion's *demands* at Smithfield were not debt-cancellation demands; they were labour and legal demands. So debt was a cause but not a rallying cry. Rating 2.

## Pattern notes

- Debt and [[wars]] are usually the same chain of causation in pre-modern rebellions. A war is financed by borrowing; the borrowing is repaid by taxation; the taxation falls on the people who didn't want the war.
- Debt-jubilee rebellions (intensity 3) are rare in the historical record. The most famous is the Solonian reforms in ancient Athens (6th century BC), which formally cancelled debts and freed debt-slaves. These are structurally different from economic rebellions — they target a specific legal instrument rather than conditions.
