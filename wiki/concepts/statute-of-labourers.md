# Statute of Labourers (1351)

An English act of Parliament passed in 1351 in response to the labour market consequences of the [[black-death]]. It froze wages at pre-plague levels and made it illegal for peasants to leave one manor in search of better pay at another. It was the primary long-term grievance that led to the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] thirty years later.

## What the law did

- **Froze wages** at the levels of 1346 (two years before the plague)
- **Made it illegal for labourers to refuse work** offered at the pre-plague wage rate
- **Made it illegal for employers to pay more** than the pre-plague rate
- **Made it illegal for peasants to leave their manor** to seek better pay elsewhere
- Imposed **fines on both employers and labourers** who violated the provisions
- Was enforced by special commissions known as **Justices of Labourers**

## Why it was passed

The [[black-death]] had killed a third to a half of England's population in 1348–1351. The surviving labour force could, in a free market, command significantly higher wages than before. In many places they did. For landowners — including the Parliament that wrote the Statute — this was an economic catastrophe: their rents and labour costs were both pressed against them from opposite directions.

The Statute was Parliament's attempt to **legislate the Black Death's economic consequences out of existence**. It was, as the channel's thesis holds, an attempt to hold a legal structure against a physical reality. It could not work.

## Why it was hated

- **Enforcement was uneven.** Wealthy landowners could often evade it or bribe the Justices. Small farmers who wanted to pay more to attract workers were penalized harshly.
- **It punished both the labourer and the employer,** which meant peasants couldn't easily find legal cover in a sympathetic lord.
- **It was an obvious class measure.** Nobody pretended it was anything else. The rebellion's leaders in 1381 named it explicitly in their grievances.
- It preserved [[villeinage]] — the formal institution of serfdom — against a labour market that was making serfdom obsolete

## Why it ultimately failed

Not because it was repealed. It was not repealed for centuries. It failed because **the underlying economic pressure won**. Peasants moved anyway. Employers paid more anyway, under the table. Lords who refused to compete for labour lost their workers to lords who did. Over the course of the 15th century, the Statute became a dead letter — a law still on the books but unenforceable in practice.

This is the canonical illustration of [[structural-vs-tactical-victory]]: the Peasants' Revolt lost in 1381, the Statute was reaffirmed, and yet within a century the thing the statute was trying to preserve — unfree labour tied to the manor — was effectively dead. The market had won.
