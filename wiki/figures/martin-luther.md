# Martin Luther

German monk, theologian, and founder of the Protestant Reformation. A central — and genuinely ambivalent — figure in the [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]].

## What we know

- Born in Eisleben in 1483. An Augustinian friar and professor of theology at Wittenberg.
- Posted the Ninety-Five Theses against indulgences in 1517. Excommunicated 1521.
- Published the German New Testament in 1522 — putting Scripture in the hands of ordinary literate and semi-literate Germans for the first time. This is the precondition for peasants reading the Bible themselves and noticing the absence of mandatory tithes to absentee bishops.
- In April 1525 he wrote the *Admonition to Peace*, criticising both princes and peasants and urging non-violent reform.
- In May 1525, as the peasant armies advanced and [[thomas-muntzer]] led the Thuringian wing under a rainbow banner, he wrote the pamphlet retitled by printers as [[../concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525|*Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants*]], urging the princes to *"stab, smite, slay, whoever can."*
- Within a year of endorsing the peasants' biblical grievances, he had endorsed their massacre in writing.

## Significance

Luther's trajectory across 1525 is one of the most cited "the reformer chooses the prince over the people" moments in European history. The long-term effect was that the Reformation bound itself to princely authority in order to survive, which helped ensure that the territories where Lutheranism won also saw feudal obligations on peasants *tightened* rather than loosened in the century that followed.

This is the central complication of [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] as applied to 1525: the most influential voice in the empire, on the peasants' side in April, was on the princes' side by May, and the structural consequences followed.
