# Theme — Royalty / Leadership

One of the six recurring conditions. Royalty covers **legitimacy crises, succession disputes, absent or weak rulers, visible excess, and the question of whether rebels frame the crown itself as the problem**.

## How to rate intensity (0–3)

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **0** | Crown is stable, respected, uncontroversial |
| **1** | Rebels attack the ruler's *advisors* rather than the ruler — the crown itself is not the target |
| **2** | A succession or legitimacy dispute is a contributing cause |
| **3** | Rebels explicitly target the monarch, dynasty, or the concept of monarchy itself |

## What to look for

- **Who do the rebels blame by name?** If the target is "bad advisors" and not the ruler, intensity is **low** (1). If the target is the ruler personally or the institution of monarchy, intensity is **high** (3).
- **Age and competence of the ruler.** A child monarch (or a mad one, or an absent one) creates a leadership vacuum that advisors fill, and advisors are easier to hate than the crown.
- **Visible excess.** Palace-building during famine. Hunting parks expanded while forests are closed to peasants. The symbolic wealth gap.

## Pattern notes

- Low royalty intensity is the rule, not the exception, in pre-modern rebellions. Most rebels before the 18th century attacked advisors rather than the crown itself — the legitimacy of monarchy as an institution was rarely questioned until the Enlightenment.
- The phrase *"bad advisors hiding the truth from the king"* recurs across centuries. It's a face-saving frame that lets rebels attack the government without committing the unthinkable (killing a divinely ordained ruler).
- When royalty intensity is **high** (3), you are usually looking at a post-Enlightenment rebellion or a contested succession.
