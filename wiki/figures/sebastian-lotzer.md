# Sebastian Lotzer

A journeyman furrier and lay preacher from Memmingen who, between 27 February and 1 March 1525, drafted the text of the [[../concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles of the Swabian Peasants]] — one of the first mass-printed political manifestos in European history.

## What we know

- A working-class artisan (furrier), not a cleric or a noble.
- Part of the reformist circle around the Memmingen preacher [[christoph-schappeler]], who is generally credited with writing the preamble and theological justification to the Articles.
- Distilled more than three hundred separate peasant grievance lists from Upper Swabian bands into twelve crisp, Scripture-cited demands.
- Around 20 March 1525 the assembled peasant delegates in Memmingen formally adopted the Articles and the Federal Order.
- What happened to him after the rebellion was crushed is unclear; he disappears from the record.

## Significance

Lotzer is a reminder that the [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]] produced original political theory from people outside the clerical and noble classes — something that the standard "Luther and princes" framing of 1525 tends to erase. The Twelve Articles, reprinted at least 25 times in two months, were drafted by a furrier and a small-town preacher, and they anticipate themes that will reappear in every popular manifesto for the next three centuries.
