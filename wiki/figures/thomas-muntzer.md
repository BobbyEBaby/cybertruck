# Thomas Müntzer

Radical German reformer, former associate and later theological opponent of [[martin-luther]], and spiritual leader of the Thuringian peasant army crushed at the [[../concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen|Battle of Frankenhausen]] on 15 May 1525.

## What we know

- Born in Stolberg around 1489; educated in Leipzig and Frankfurt-on-Oder.
- Initially drawn to the Lutheran Reformation but broke with Luther over Luther's alliance with the princes and refusal to push reform into social and economic structures.
- Preached a fiery apocalyptic theology: the last days were at hand, the godless must be overthrown, and the common people were God's instrument.
- Arrived at Frankenhausen on 11 May 1525 with about 300 fighters from Mühlhausen, joining several thousand peasants already camped there under a banner of the rainbow.
- On 14–15 May, the peasant army was destroyed by professional troops of [[philip-of-hesse]] and Duke George of Saxony. Around 7,000 peasants died; Landsknecht losses were reported as six.
- Captured in the town of Frankenhausen after the battle, tortured for nearly two weeks at Heldrungen, and beheaded at Mühlhausen on 27 May 1525. His head was impaled on a pike and displayed publicly for months.

## Significance

Müntzer is [[martin-luther]]'s shadow: the reformer who took the "priesthood of all believers" doctrine and asked why it should not apply to land, tithes, and princes as well as to churches. He is the figure whose existence forced Luther to choose sides — and Luther chose the princes, in writing, in [[../concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525|*Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants*]].

Later generations — the 17th-century radicals, Friedrich Engels in *Der deutsche Bauernkrieg* (1850), the 20th-century East German state — each rewrote Müntzer in their own image. The historical man remains harder to see than the myth.
