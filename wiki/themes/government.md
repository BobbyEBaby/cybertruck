# Theme — Government

One of the six recurring conditions. Government covers **corruption, failed justice, denied representation, bureaucratic overreach, and the loss of legitimacy of the administrative apparatus (as distinct from the crown itself — see [[royalty]])**.

## How to rate intensity (0–3)

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **0** | Government is functional and broadly trusted |
| **1** | Some grievances (corrupt local officials, unjust verdicts) but the system mostly works |
| **2** | Significant and visible administrative failure contributing to the rebellion |
| **3** | Total collapse of administrative legitimacy — the system is seen as the problem |

## What to look for

- **How does the government respond to complaints?** If the answer is "more enforcement, more collectors, more courts," intensity is high.
- **Is the legal apparatus trusted?** A law that exists but is universally ignored is different from a law that is enforced unfairly. The latter is more explosive.
- **Do rebels make specific demands for governmental reform** — as opposed to only demanding economic relief? Demands like "execute the traitors against the crown" or "abolish villeinage" are classic government-theme grievances.

## Observations across rebellions

### [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] — intensity **3/3**
The [[../concepts/poll-tax-1381]] was seen as corrupt, unjustly applied, and tied to a failing war. The government's response to peasant resistance was to send *more* collectors, not fewer. The rebels' specific demands at Smithfield — end of villeinage, end of forest law, pardon for rebels, execution of "traitors against the crown" — were all governmental reforms, not economic ones. When they stormed London, the first institutional target was the Temple (legal records storehouse), which they burned. The second was the Tower of London, from which they dragged [[../figures/simon-sudbury]] and [[../figures/robert-hales]] for public execution. Maximum intensity.

## Pattern notes

- Government failure and [[economy]] failure almost always co-occur. A government that is competent about its finances can absorb an economic shock without losing legitimacy; a government already failing administratively cannot.
- The burning of legal records is a recurring motif across government-theme rebellions. When rebels burn the tax rolls or the court archives, they are attacking the *memory* of the system that oppresses them — not just the current officials.
