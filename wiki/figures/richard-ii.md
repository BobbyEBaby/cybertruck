# Richard II

King of England from 1377 to 1399, grandson of Edward III, son of Edward the Black Prince. Fourteen years old during the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] and effectively ruled by the council of his uncle [[john-of-gaunt]].

## Role in the Peasants' Revolt

- Formally on the throne but too young to govern; real power was held by [[john-of-gaunt]] and the king's council
- The rebels did not blame Richard personally — their fury was directed at his advisors, especially [[john-of-gaunt]], [[simon-sudbury]], and Robert Hales
- Genuinely trapped in London during the rebellion's climax, with almost no loyal military force inside the city walls
- Sent word that he would meet the rebels at Smithfield on June 15, 1381
- At Smithfield, after [[wat-tyler]] was stabbed by [[william-walworth]] and the crowd drew their bows, Richard rode alone toward the rebels and, according to chronicler Thomas Walsingham, said: *"Sirs, will you shoot your king? I will be your captain. You shall have from me that which you seek."*
- The crowd, stunned, followed him peacefully out of the city
- Within days, Richard's council mobilized the London militia and began hunting down the rebel leaders
- Within weeks, every promise he had made at Smithfield was formally revoked
- When villeins reminded him of his word, he reportedly replied: *"Villeins you were, and villeins you are. In bondage you shall remain."*

## Why the [[../themes/royalty]] intensity for 1381 is only 1/3

Richard was never the target of the rebels' anger. This is unusual for a 14/18 rebellion — in most cases of extreme pressure the crown itself comes under threat. In 1381 the monarchy *as an institution* was never questioned; it was simply believed to be held hostage by bad advisors.

This was face-saving for the rebels (attacking the king personally was unthinkable) and it was what ultimately let the rebellion be dispersed — when Richard rode out alone, the crowd's loyalty to the crown-as-institution outweighed their loyalty to Wat Tyler.

## Later life

- Reigned another 18 years after 1381
- Deposed by his cousin Henry Bolingbroke in 1399 (who became Henry IV)
- Died in captivity at Pontefract Castle in February 1400, almost certainly murdered

The man who had ridden alone toward 20,000 drawn bows at age 14 was killed at 33 by his own cousin, far from any battlefield.
