# William Walworth

Lord Mayor of London during the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]]. The man who stabbed [[wat-tyler]] at the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381#The Smithfield parley June 15 1381|Smithfield parley]] on June 15, 1381.

## Role in the Peasants' Revolt

- A wealthy fishmonger and one of the most influential merchants in London
- Serving his second term as Lord Mayor in 1381
- Rode in [[richard-ii]]'s small retinue to Smithfield on June 15
- During the parley, after [[wat-tyler]]'s confrontational behavior toward the king, rode forward and stabbed Tyler in the neck
- Tyler fell from his horse and was finished off by others in the retinue
- Walworth then rode back into London, raised the city militia, and returned in time to escort the king safely out of Smithfield after the crowd had followed Richard
- Knighted by Richard II in the field at Smithfield that same day — one of only a handful of men knighted for a single act rather than a career of service

## Significance

Walworth is a reminder that rebellions are not crushed by great armies. They are often crushed by a small group of men with short swords who act faster than anyone expects. [[wat-tyler]] came to Smithfield to negotiate; Walworth came to kill him. The asymmetry of intent is what ended the rebellion.

This is also a quiet counter-example to the clean hero/villain framing that rebellion stories usually attract. Walworth is sometimes celebrated in English civic history as the savior of the crown, and sometimes reviled as the assassin of the people's champion. Both readings are coherent, and the channel's position is that neither is more true than the other — **he was a rich merchant defending the order that had made him rich**.
