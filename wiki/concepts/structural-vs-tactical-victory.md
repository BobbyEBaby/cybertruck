# Structural vs. tactical victory

The founding thesis of Popular Uprisings as a channel. Stated in full:

> **Rebellions rarely win in their own time. But the conditions that cause them almost always win in the century that follows.**

## The claim

A rebellion can be judged on two timescales:

- **Tactical timescale** — what happened in the days, weeks, or months of the rebellion itself. Did the rebels take power? Did they win their demands? Did their leaders survive? Were their promises honored? This is how contemporaneous chroniclers usually measured success.
- **Structural timescale** — what happened in the decades or century that followed. Did the underlying conditions that produced the rebellion change? Did the institution the rebels were attacking survive? Did the legal or economic regime the rebels were denouncing still exist a generation later?

The thesis is: **rebellions almost always lose on the tactical timescale and almost always win on the structural timescale.** Not because of the rebellion itself (which is usually crushed and forgotten), but because the pressure that produced the rebellion doesn't go away when the rebels do. The pressure has to go somewhere. And over the long run, the thing the rebels were pushing against — the unjust tax, the unworkable law, the unsustainable institution — usually gives way, quietly, without anyone calling it a victory.

## Why it might be true

The six themes Popular Uprisings tracks ([[../themes/economy]], [[../themes/royalty]], [[../themes/government]], [[../themes/wars]], [[../themes/environment]], [[../themes/debt]]) are **structural**. They are not opinions. They are pressures. When the pressure exceeds what the system can absorb, a rebellion breaks out. Crushing the rebellion does not relieve the pressure. The pressure is still there.

Over time, the system has to adapt to the pressure OR the pressure has to be reduced by something exogenous (a new war removes the surplus population, a new plague removes the surplus pressure, etc.). In most historical cases neither of those happens cleanly, and the system adapts — quietly, through a thousand individual decisions, without any single moment that can be called the victory.

## Where we'll test it

This page is the central hypothesis the channel is built around. Every new rebellion in the [[../index|wiki]] should be tagged with a tactical outcome (`crushed`, `partial_win`, `full_win`, `triggered_reform`) and a structural outcome (100-year follow-up: did the conditions still hold, or had they yielded?). Over many episodes we should be able to test the thesis empirically.

## Counter-cases we should be honest about

The thesis is bold. It might be wrong in several ways:

- **Some rebellions are crushed and the conditions persist.** The 1871 Paris Commune was crushed; the conditions that produced it (industrial precarity, urban inequality) are arguably still with us 150 years later. The structural victory may take much longer than "a century" in some cases.
- **Some rebellions cause reactionary entrenchment.** The failed 1848 European revolutions were followed by decades of more aggressive state control, not by the liberalization the rebels sought. The pressure didn't go away, but the system's response was to clamp down harder, not to yield.
- **"Structural victory" is slippery.** If we define the thesis so broadly that any eventual change counts as a structural victory, the thesis becomes unfalsifiable. We need to keep the bar concrete: the specific institution or law the rebels targeted has to actually go away.

Future episodes will flag counter-cases honestly in place rather than silently excluding them.

## Observations

### [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]]
The canonical example. The rebellion was crushed within 24 hours of its high point. [[../figures/wat-tyler]] was murdered. Every promise [[../figures/richard-ii]] made at Smithfield was revoked within weeks. On the tactical timescale, total defeat.

And yet — within 100 years, [[villeinage]] was effectively dead in England. Not because the crown changed its mind. Because the labour market made the [[statute-of-labourers]] unenforceable. The peasants, quietly, individually, moved anyway. Wages rose anyway. The structural pressure — created by the [[black-death]] — won.

**Tactical outcome:** crushed.
**Structural outcome:** won (villeinage dead by ~1500, though the precise date is debated).
