# Wat Tyler

Leader of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]]. A man about whom almost nothing is known before June 1381, and who was dead by June 15 of that same year — but who, for about ten days, stood at the front of the largest popular uprising in medieval English history.

## What we know

- **Probably a roofer from Kent** — the word "tyler" was an occupational surname meaning "one who lays roof tiles," and Kent tax rolls from 1379 show a Walter Tyler in the right region.
- He emerged as the rebels' leader around June 10, 1381, as the uprising spread from Kent and Essex toward London.
- He led the rebel host into London on June 12–13, directing the burning of the Savoy Palace and the storming of the Tower.
- At the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381#The Smithfield parley June 15 1381|Smithfield parley]] on June 15, he rode forward to speak directly with [[richard-ii]].
- He was stabbed by [[william-walworth]], the Lord Mayor of London, at Smithfield. The immediate cause is disputed — some chroniclers say Tyler insulted one of the king's retinue, others that he reached for his dagger.
- He was dead by nightfall.

## What we do NOT know

- His birth year
- His family situation (wife, children — nothing attested)
- His literacy (probably illiterate, but some rebel leaders were not — this is unsettled)
- Whether he had a coherent political program before the rebellion, or whether his demands at Smithfield were improvised
- Whether "Wat Tyler" was even his real name or a nom de guerre

## Significance

Wat Tyler is a useful illustration of a recurring pattern in rebellion history: **the leader who walks into the light for a few days and is immediately killed**. His biography is almost entirely the rebellion itself. There is very little "before" and no "after."

This is one reason the [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] thesis holds: leaders like Wat Tyler can be cheaply eliminated, but the conditions that elevated them — [[../themes/economy]] pressure, [[../themes/environment]] shock, [[../themes/government]] failure — cannot.
