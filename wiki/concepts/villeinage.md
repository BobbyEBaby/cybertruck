# Villeinage

The legal status of a villein — a peasant bound to a manor and its lord by hereditary unfree tenure. Villeinage was the English form of serfdom and was the formal legal institution that the rebels of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] explicitly demanded be abolished.

## What it meant in practice

A villein was technically free in body (not a slave) but bound to the land of a specific manor. A villein could not:

- Leave the manor without the lord's permission
- Marry without the lord's permission (or without paying a fine called *merchet*)
- Inherit freely — the lord took a fine called *heriot* on the death of a villein father
- Be tried in the king's courts for most disputes — manorial courts had jurisdiction
- Own land outright; the land a villein "held" was held from the lord in return for labour services and rents

A villein *could*:

- Own moveable property (tools, livestock, a modest house)
- Sue other villeins in the manorial court
- Pass holdings to children, subject to the lord's fines
- Become free by escaping to a chartered town and remaining there for a year and a day (a famous legal principle often invoked and rarely achieved)

## Why it was the rebels' top demand

At the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381#The Smithfield parley June 15 1381|Smithfield parley]] on June 15, 1381, [[../figures/wat-tyler]]'s first demand of [[../figures/richard-ii]] was the **abolition of villeinage**. Not lower taxes, not better wages — the end of the legal institution entirely.

Why? Because the rebels understood that the [[statute-of-labourers]] and the [[poll-tax-1381]] were possible *only because* they were villeins. Free labourers could not be held to frozen wages. Free labourers could not be taxed per head for a war they had not consented to. Abolishing villeinage would have pulled the legal rug out from under every other grievance.

Richard agreed. Within weeks the agreement was revoked, the rebels were hunted down, and villeinage was formally reaffirmed.

## How it actually died

Villeinage was never formally abolished in England. It simply became **unenforceable** over the course of the 15th century. Lords who tried to hold their villeins to the land found themselves outcompeted by lords who paid wages to attract workers. Villeins who moved to towns or took work on other manors were rarely pursued. Manorial courts quietly stopped collecting *merchet* and *heriot*. By 1500, the institution was effectively extinct in practice, though the formal legal status lingered in rare cases into the 17th century.

This is the single clearest illustration of [[structural-vs-tactical-victory]] in the channel's entire subject matter. The rebels demanded abolition in 1381. The crown refused and reaffirmed. And within a century, the thing the rebels had demanded — the actual disappearance of unfree peasant tenure from English life — had happened anyway. Not because any law changed. Because the labour market, shaped by the [[black-death]], made the institution unmaintainable.
