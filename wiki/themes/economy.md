# Theme — Economy

One of the six recurring conditions Popular Uprisings tracks across every rebellion. Economy covers **inflation, price shocks, food scarcity, taxation, wealth concentration, and laws that try to hold wages or prices against market pressure**.

## How to rate intensity (0–3)

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **0** | No significant economic grievance |
| **1** | Present as background — cost of living, tax burden, but not a proximate cause |
| **2** | A significant contributing grievance, visible in the rebellion's stated demands |
| **3** | The dominant cause — the rebellion is *about* the economy |

## What to look for

- **Laws trying to fix prices or wages against the underlying market** (e.g. the [[../concepts/statute-of-labourers]] after the [[../concepts/black-death]]). These are the clearest single signal.
- **A sudden shock** — crop failure, currency debasement, tax increase — applied to a population that was previously just-about-coping
- **Wealth concentration visible to the underclass** — palaces being built while people starve
- **A tax imposed flat regardless of ability to pay** (see [[../concepts/poll-tax-1381]])

## Observations across rebellions

### [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] — intensity **3/3**
The [[../concepts/statute-of-labourers]] (1351) was an explicit, visible, hated attempt to freeze wages below what the post-plague market would pay. Every peasant marching on London in 1381 knew what the law was and whose interest it served. Purest form of the theme possible.

## Pattern notes

- High economy intensity does NOT guarantee a rebellion. It's a necessary condition in most cases, not a sufficient one. Look for it in combination with [[government]] failure and [[environment]] shocks.
- Economy-driven rebellions almost always **lose tactically and win structurally** (see [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]]) — markets are pressure, and pressure eventually wins against laws that deny them.
