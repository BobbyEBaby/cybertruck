# John of Gaunt

Duke of Lancaster. Third surviving son of Edward III. Uncle of [[richard-ii]]. The man the rebels of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] blamed for every grievance they had — despite the fact that he was not even in southern England when they rose.

## Role in the Peasants' Revolt

- By 1381, as the king was only 14, John of Gaunt was the most powerful man in England in practice if not in title
- He was widely associated with unpopular policies: the failing Hundred Years' War, the high taxes, the aggressive prosecution of villeinage, and his own massive personal wealth
- He was on campaign in Scotland when the rebellion broke out — physically beyond the rebels' reach, which may have saved his life
- The rebels burned his **Savoy Palace** in London to the ground during the June 13 uprising. According to the chroniclers, they smashed every gold and silver object in the building rather than looting anything, specifically because they wanted to be seen as rebels and not thieves
- The Savoy was probably the richest private residence in England; its destruction was the single most valuable property loss in English history up to that point

## Why he was the rebels' focal target

[[richard-ii]] was 14 and personally untargetable (see [[../themes/royalty]] intensity 1/3). [[simon-sudbury]] was the name on the tax. But John of Gaunt was the *symbol* — the visible excess, the wealth concentration, the failing war, the council that ran the king. He was the closest the rebels could come to attacking the system without attacking the crown itself.

This is a recurring pattern Popular Uprisings will see in future episodes: when the monarch is untouchable (too young, too loved, or too far), rebels pick a proxy — a powerful regent, a royal favorite, an archbishop — and concentrate their rage there. The proxy becomes the lightning rod that protects the throne.

## After 1381

John of Gaunt outlived the rebellion by 18 years, eventually dying in 1399 — a few months before his own son, Henry Bolingbroke, deposed [[richard-ii]] and took the throne as Henry IV, founding the House of Lancaster. The dynasty that ruled England for the next 60 years was John of Gaunt's legacy, not his nephew's.
