# The Black Death

The second plague pandemic to strike Europe, arriving in England in 1348 and running to approximately 1351. The single largest environmental shock in European history between the fall of Rome and the 20th century.

## Scale

- Killed somewhere between **a third and a half of the English population**. Estimates vary; 40% is a common single figure, but credible estimates run 30%–60%.
- Killed comparable proportions across the rest of Europe — perhaps 75–200 million people total.
- Recurred in waves through the late 14th century, keeping mortality high for a generation
- Left **whole villages empty**, farms untended, labour catastrophically scarce

## Why it matters for Popular Uprisings

The Black Death is the canonical example of the [[../themes/environment]] theme operating at maximum intensity. It is the upstream cause of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] through a chain of second-order effects:

1. Plague kills ~40% of the population
2. Surviving peasants' labour suddenly becomes more valuable (fewer workers, same amount of land)
3. Wages start to rise
4. Parliament panics and passes the [[statute-of-labourers]] (1351) to freeze wages at pre-plague levels
5. The statute is resented, enforced unevenly, and creates three decades of slow pressure
6. The 1381 [[poll-tax-1381]] is the trigger that converts the slow pressure into an open rebellion

Without the Black Death, there is no Peasants' Revolt. The rebels didn't think they were reacting to a plague from 30 years earlier — they thought they were reacting to a tax and a set of hated advisors. But the conditions they were reacting to were downstream of the plague, every one of them.

## Structural lesson

The Black Death is the clearest illustration of why [[structural-vs-tactical-victory]] holds in pre-modern rebellions. You can crush the peasants in a field. You cannot bring back the dead population. The labour market's new reality is permanent, and any law that tries to deny it will eventually fail.
