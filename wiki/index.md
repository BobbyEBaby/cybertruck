## Rebellions

- [[rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] — England, 1381. Post-Black-Death labour-shortage rebellion against the Poll Tax and the Statute of Labourers. Intensity 14/18. Outcome: `partial_win` (crushed tactically, won structurally over ~100 years).
- [[rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]] — Holy Roman Empire, 1524–1525. The largest popular uprising in Europe before the French Revolution. ≈300,000 peasants rose, ≈100,000 killed. Crushed at [[concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen|Frankenhausen]] on 15 May 1525. Intensity 12/18. Outcome: `crushed` — the first case where [[concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] meets genuine trouble (see [[concepts/second-serfdom]]).

## Figures

### 1381 — Peasants' Revolt
- [[figures/wat-tyler]] — Kent roofer. Led the 1381 Peasants' Revolt. Murdered at Smithfield by [[figures/william-walworth]].
- [[figures/richard-ii]] — King of England 1377–1399. 14 years old during the Peasants' Revolt. Reigned by the council of his uncle [[figures/john-of-gaunt]].
- [[figures/william-walworth]] — Lord Mayor of London 1381. Killed [[figures/wat-tyler]] at Smithfield.
- [[figures/simon-sudbury]] — Archbishop of Canterbury and Lord Chancellor of England. Beheaded by rebels on Tower Hill, June 1381.
- [[figures/john-of-gaunt]] — Uncle of [[figures/richard-ii]]. Effective ruler of England in 1381. Target of the rebels' hatred; his Savoy Palace was burned.

### 1524–1525 — German Peasants' War
- [[figures/thomas-muntzer]] — Radical reformer and spiritual leader of the Thuringian wing. Captured at [[concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen|Frankenhausen]], tortured, beheaded at Mühlhausen on 27 May 1525.
- [[figures/martin-luther]] — Reformer. Endorsed the [[concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles]] as biblical in April 1525 and called for the peasants' massacre in May 1525 with [[concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525|*Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants*]].
- [[figures/sebastian-lotzer]] — Memmingen furrier. Principal author of the body text of the [[concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles]].
- [[figures/christoph-schappeler]] — Memmingen preacher. Author of the preamble to the [[concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles]].
- [[figures/philip-of-hesse]] — Landgrave of Hesse. Protestant prince. Joint commander of the force that destroyed [[figures/thomas-muntzer]]'s army at [[concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen|Frankenhausen]].
- [[figures/charles-v]] — Holy Roman Emperor 1519–1556. Absent in Spain during the Peasants' War; never named as an enemy by the rebels. The reason 1525's [[themes/royalty]] intensity is low.

## Concepts

### Channel thesis
- [[concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] — the channel's central thesis: rebellions rarely win in their own moment, but the conditions that cause them *usually* win in the century that follows. First seriously complicated by the [[rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524|1525 case]] via [[concepts/second-serfdom]].

### 1381-specific
- [[concepts/black-death]] — the 1348–1351 plague that killed ~40% of England's population and created the labour-market conditions for the 1381 revolt.
- [[concepts/statute-of-labourers]] — the 1351 English law that froze wages at pre-plague levels, the primary economic grievance of the Peasants' Revolt.
- [[concepts/poll-tax-1381]] — the third successive poll tax, imposed at 12 pence per head regardless of income, the proximate trigger of the Peasants' Revolt.
- [[concepts/villeinage]] — the legal status of serfs bound to a manor. Formally preserved by the crown after 1381; quietly dead within 100 years.

### 1525-specific
- [[concepts/twelve-articles-1525]] — the polite, Scripture-cited manifesto of the Swabian peasants, drafted in Memmingen in late February – early March 1525. One of the first mass-printed political documents in European history (≈25,000 copies in two months).
- [[concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen]] — the decisive engagement of the Thuringian phase, 14–15 May 1525. Roughly 7,000 peasant dead versus ≈6 Landsknecht losses. A massacre, not a battle.
- [[concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525]] — Luther's May 1525 pamphlet urging the princes to *"stab, smite, slay"* the peasants. The moment the Reformation bound itself to princely power.
- [[concepts/second-serfdom]] — the historians' term for the tightening of serf-like obligations east of the Elbe after 1525. The structural counterexample to [[concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]].
