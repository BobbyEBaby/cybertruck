# German Peasants' War of 1524–1525

A widespread popular revolt across the Holy Roman Empire from summer 1524 through the reprisal phase of 1526–27, involving an estimated 300,000 peasants in arms, roughly 100,000 of whom were killed. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War --> It was the largest popular uprising in Europe before the French Revolution almost 270 years later. The revolt produced the [[../concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles of the Swabian Peasants]], one of the first mass-printed political manifestos in European history, and was crushed decisively at the [[../concepts/battle-of-frankenhausen|Battle of Frankenhausen]] on 15 May 1525 by the professional Landsknecht troops of [[../figures/philip-of-hesse]] and George of Saxony. [[../figures/martin-luther]] notoriously shifted from cautious support in his *Admonition to Peace* (April 1525) to a blood-curdling call for massacre in *[[../concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525|Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants]]* (May 1525). The first rebellion where this wiki's [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] thesis meets genuine trouble.

## Causes — the conditions that had to be in place

Across the decades before 1524, feudal dues in much of southern Germany were rising, not falling. New silver from the mines of Tyrol and Bohemia was flooding Europe and lifting prices; lords were passing the cost onto peasants by raising rents, reintroducing personal serfdom on paper, enclosing common land for hunting reserves, and collecting tithes more aggressively. <!-- source: https://www.history.ox.ac.uk/article/the-german-peasants-war-15241525 --> Tithes in particular had become financialized — sold as a yield-bearing asset to absentee landlords who took a one-tenth cut of every harvest.

[[../figures/martin-luther]]'s Reformation provided the match. His German New Testament (1522) put Scripture into the hands of ordinary literate and semi-literate peasants for the first time, and the absence of any New Testament warrant for compulsory tithes to an absentee bishop became impossible to unsee. Luther did not call for rebellion. But he had been telling Germans, for seven years, that no human authority stands between them and God.

## The events

### The Twelve Articles (late February – March 1525)

In the free city of Memmingen, [[../figures/sebastian-lotzer]] and [[../figures/christoph-schappeler]] distilled more than three hundred separate peasant grievance lists into a single document: the [[../concepts/twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles of the Swabian Peasants]]. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Articles --> Printed in Augsburg and reprinted at least 25 times in two months (≈25,000 copies, enormous for the era), the Articles opened with the right to elect a community pastor, demanded that the great tithe go to that pastor rather than to absentee financiers, called the little tithe unscriptural and to be abolished, and went on to demand the restoration of common lands, an end to reintroduced serfdom, and fair justice. Every article cited Scripture. The document was polite — startlingly so.

### Luther's two pamphlets (April–May 1525)

In April 1525 Luther published the *Admonition to Peace*, telling the princes their greed had brought this on themselves and telling the peasants that most of the Twelve Articles were biblical but they must not take up arms. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Against_the_Murderous,_Thieving_Hordes_of_Peasants --> It pleased almost no one. In May, with news of burning monasteries and [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]]'s radical army under its rainbow banner, he wrote a second pamphlet. Printers retitled it, without his consent, *[[../concepts/against-the-murderous-thieving-hordes-1525|Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants]]*, and in it Luther told the princes that a rebel was like a mad dog that must be struck down: *"stab, smite, slay, whoever can."*

### Frankenhausen (14–15 May 1525)

[[../figures/thomas-muntzer]] had arrived at Frankenhausen on 11 May with about 300 fighters from Mühlhausen; several thousand peasants of the surrounding Thuringian and Saxon estates were already camped in the fields. On 14 and 15 May the professional troops of [[../figures/philip-of-hesse]] and Duke George of Saxony opened with an artillery barrage, then cavalry and Landsknechts caught the peasants off guard. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Frankenhausen --> Around 7,000 peasants died. Landsknecht losses were reported as six. Müntzer was captured in the town, tortured for nearly two weeks, and beheaded at Mühlhausen on 27 May. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_M%C3%BCntzer -->

### The reprisals (1525–1527)

Across the rest of 1525 and into 1527, every major peasant army was hunted down and broken. The total death toll — peasants, civilians, soldiers — came to roughly 100,000 out of perhaps 300,000 who had risen. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War -->

## Why it matters

This is the first episode where [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]] meets real trouble.

The German Peasants' War was crushed tactically: ≈100,000 dead, every major peasant army destroyed, the Twelve Articles' demands rejected and the leaders hunted. That part matches 1381.

But then the structural story diverges. In southern Germany — roughly, the regions where the rebellion had been thickest — serfdom did slowly weaken over the following centuries and the financial form of the great tithe became harder for absentee landlords to defend. A weak, slow, partial structural win. **But in East Prussia and the territories east of the Elbe, the princes went the other direction entirely: they introduced what historians call *the [[../concepts/second-serfdom|second serfdom]]*, tightening peasant obligations over the following century.** <!-- source: https://jacobin.com/2023/12/german-peasants-war-feudalism-class-conflict-reformation --> Rent burdens in some areas reached 60–80% of harvest by the 1570s, helping to drive the hunger-and-plague cycle that fed into the Thirty Years War.

The [[../figures/martin-luther|Luther]] trajectory compounds this. His Reformation ended up bound to princely power in order to survive — which is another way of saying that the peasants lost their natural ally, because Luther and his successors needed the princes more than the princes needed them. <!-- source: https://witness.lcms.org/2025/when-the-lord-has-not-spoken-what-the-german-peasants-war-teaches-about-political-life-today/ -->

> **Contradiction with [[peasants-revolt-1381]]:** In England 1381, the rebellion was crushed tactically and won structurally over the following century — the clean illustration of [[../concepts/structural-vs-tactical-victory]]. In Germany 1525, the rebellion was crushed tactically and then, in much of the empire, *also* lost structurally: serfdom was tightened, not weakened, for another ≈300 years east of the Elbe. The channel's thesis survives as a *tendency*, not a law. Both cases are real history; neither explains the other away.
