# Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants

A pamphlet written by [[../figures/martin-luther]] in May 1525 during the climax of the [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]], urging the German princes to crush the peasant rebellion without mercy. Its short form became one of the most notorious pieces of writing of the Reformation. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Against_the_Murderous,_Thieving_Hordes_of_Peasants -->

## Background

In April 1525 Luther had published the *Admonition to Peace*, criticising both the princes (for greed) and the peasants (for taking the sword) and urging non-violent reform. He said most of the demands in the [[twelve-articles-1525|Twelve Articles]] were just and biblical. The pamphlet pleased almost no one.

Then the peasant risings spread, monasteries were sacked, and [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]] led an army under a rainbow banner toward Frankenhausen. Luther wrote a second pamphlet, originally titled *Against the Rioting Peasants*. Printers in other cities retitled it, without his consent, *Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants* — and that harsher title stuck.

## The key passage

> *"Therefore, dear lords, here is a place where you can release, rescue, help. Have mercy on these poor people. Let whoever can stab, smite, slay. If you die in doing it, good for you! A more blessed death can never be yours, for you die while obeying the divine word and commandment."*

It is genuinely shocking writing. Luther is urging Christian princes that killing peasants in this moment is a *blessed* act — better, for a prince's soul, than dying a normal death.

## Timing

- **19 April 1525** — *Admonition to Peace* published. Moderate, critical of both sides.
- **early May 1525** — Peasant armies advance. Monasteries burn. Müntzer's army assembles in Thuringia.
- **c. 10 May 1525** — Luther writes the second pamphlet.
- **14–15 May 1525** — [[battle-of-frankenhausen|Battle of Frankenhausen]]. ≈7,000 peasants killed.
- **27 May 1525** — [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]] beheaded at Mühlhausen.
- **later 1525** — Luther writes *An Open Letter on the Harsh Book Against the Peasants* to Caspar Müller, chancellor of Mansfeld, defending his language but acknowledging it had been taken further than he intended by the princes.

Within roughly one month, Luther moved from calling the Twelve Articles just and biblical to calling the peasants mad dogs that should be slaughtered.

## Why it matters

This pamphlet is the document most often cited as the moment the Reformation bound itself to princely power. The peasants had hoped Luther might be their ally; in May 1525 they learned, in writing, that he was not. The long-term structural effect in the Lutheran German states was that feudal obligations on peasants were tightened, not loosened — see [[second-serfdom]] and the discussion in [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524#Why it matters]].

It is also the single strongest piece of evidence for the complication that [[structural-vs-tactical-victory]] runs into in 1525: in England 1381, the rebellion was tactically crushed and structurally won over a century. In Germany 1525, the rebellion was tactically crushed and structurally *lost*, in large part because its most influential potential ally wrote this pamphlet.
