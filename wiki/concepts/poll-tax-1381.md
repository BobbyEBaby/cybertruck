# The Poll Tax of 1381

The third in a series of head taxes imposed by the English Parliament to finance the Hundred Years' War with France. It was the **proximate trigger** of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]].

## What the tax was

- **12 pence per head**, imposed on every lay person over the age of 14, regardless of income or wealth
- For comparison: a peasant day-labourer's wage in 1381 was about 3 pence per day. The tax was therefore roughly **four days' wages per person** — and would be collected on every member of a household.
- For a family of five adults, the tax could easily reach 60 pence — nearly a month's income for the household
- **Flat** — the Duke of Lancaster owed the same 12 pence per head as the poorest cottager
- The **third** poll tax in four years (1377, 1379, 1381), each more unpopular than the last

## Why it was uniquely hated

- **Flatness.** The earlier poll taxes had at least gestured at graduation by wealth. The 1381 tax dropped all pretense. It was, transparently, a tax designed to raise revenue from the poor because the rich had stopped paying
- **The war it funded was being lost.** After more than four decades of the Hundred Years' War, English territory in France was shrinking, not growing. The tax was paying for a defeat
- **Enforcement was corrupt.** Tax collectors were reported to be harassing women to verify their status (and extract bribes in the process). In Essex a tax collector was chased off by a crowd before formal resistance began
- **It was the third one.** Three successive poll taxes in four years was an unmistakable signal that this was not an emergency measure — it was becoming a permanent instrument

## Why it belongs to three themes at once

The Poll Tax of 1381 is one of the clearest single examples of how the Breaking Points themes overlap in practice. It was simultaneously:

- **[[../themes/economy]] (3/3)** — a flat tax on the poor
- **[[../themes/government]] (3/3)** — corrupt in enforcement, tied to a failed administrative apparatus
- **[[../themes/wars]] (2/3)** — the proximate reason the tax existed
- **[[../themes/debt]] (2/3)** — the tax was war-debt extraction by another name

One instrument, four themes. This is why the 1381 rebellion hit an intensity of 14/18 on our framework: a single trigger that embodied multiple structural failures simultaneously.

## What it actually raised

The 1381 tax raised less than expected. Widespread evasion and the rebellion that followed meant the crown did not actually collect most of what it assessed. The rebellion cost more to suppress than the tax raised.

This is a footnote worth remembering: **badly designed taxes often fail on their own terms even before they fail politically**. The Poll Tax of 1381 is one of the clearest examples in English history of a tax that damaged the legitimacy of the government, failed to raise the intended revenue, and provoked a rebellion that cost more to crush than it saved.
