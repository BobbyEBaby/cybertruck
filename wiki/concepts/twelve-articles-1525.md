# The Twelve Articles of the Swabian Peasants

A short political manifesto drafted in Memmingen between 27 February and 1 March 1525 by [[../figures/sebastian-lotzer]] (body) and [[../figures/christoph-schappeler]] (preamble), distilling more than three hundred separate peasant grievance lists from Upper Swabian bands into twelve Scripture-cited demands. Adopted by the assembled peasant delegates on 20 March 1525. Printed in Augsburg and reprinted at least 25 times in two months — roughly 25,000 copies into an era when most books sold a few hundred. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Articles -->

## The twelve demands, in plain language

1. The right of each community to elect and, if necessary, dismiss its own pastor.
2. The great tithe (grain) should be paid to the community pastor; the little tithe (livestock) should be abolished as unscriptural.
3. An end to personal serfdom: *"it is clear in the Scripture that we are free."*
4. Free rights to hunt and fish in common waters and woods.
5. Restoration of common forests seized by lords.
6. Reform of excessive compulsory labour services (Frondienste).
7. Fair, agreed rents and labour terms rather than arbitrary increases.
8. Fair rents on land — rents should reflect what the land can actually yield.
9. Predictable, regular punishments rather than arbitrary fines.
10. Restoration of common fields and meadows taken into private use by lords.
11. Abolition of *Todfall* (the lord's right to a portion of a dead peasant's property).
12. Any article shown to be unscriptural will be withdrawn — an explicit willingness to be corrected from the Bible itself.

## Why it matters

The Twelve Articles are polite, Bible-cited, and non-violent. Every demand is justified from Scripture. Every demand is modest enough that [[../figures/martin-luther]], in his April 1525 *Admonition to Peace*, said most of them were just and biblical.

This is important because it destroys the later defence that the peasants were simply out of control or nihilistic. They were not. They were making a specific, moderate, Scripture-framed set of demands — and those demands were answered with roughly 100,000 dead across the following two years.

The Articles are also one of the first mass-printed political documents in European history, and they anticipate the literary form of every popular manifesto for the next three centuries.

## Contrast with 1381

The rebels of the [[../rebellions/peasants-revolt-1381]] in England also had demands — the end of [[villeinage]], a pardon for every rebel, an end to forest law, the execution of "traitors." But those demands were delivered verbally by [[../figures/wat-tyler]] at Smithfield in a parley that lasted an hour. The Twelve Articles are a written, printed, widely distributed document. In 144 years, popular rebellion acquired the printing press.
