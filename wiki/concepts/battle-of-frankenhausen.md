# Battle of Frankenhausen

The decisive engagement of the Thuringian phase of the [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]], fought on 14–15 May 1525 near the small town of Frankenhausen in the County of Schwarzburg. Joint troops of [[../figures/philip-of-hesse]] (Landgrave of Hesse) and Duke George of Saxony destroyed the peasant army under the spiritual leadership of [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]]. <!-- source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Frankenhausen -->

## What happened

- [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]] arrived at Frankenhausen on 11 May with about 300 fighters from Mühlhausen. Several thousand peasants of the surrounding Thuringian and Saxon estates were already camped in the fields and pastures, raising perhaps 6,000–8,000 combatants in total.
- On 14–15 May the princes' force arrived with artillery, cavalry, and professional Landsknechts.
- A brief truce was negotiated while the peasants debated. During it, a rainbow appeared in the sky — Müntzer interpreted it as a divine sign and urged the peasants to hold their ground.
- The princes opened with an artillery barrage. Waves of cavalry and footsoldiers followed. The peasants, most of them armed only with pikes, flails, and improvised weapons, were caught off guard and broke. Most fled in panic toward the town.
- Mercenaries followed them into Frankenhausen and massacred them in the streets.
- Peasant dead: roughly **7,000**. Landsknecht losses: reported as **six**. The ratio tells you what kind of engagement this actually was. It was not a battle. It was a massacre.
- [[../figures/thomas-muntzer]] was captured hiding in the town, tortured for nearly two weeks at Heldrungen, and beheaded at Mühlhausen on 27 May 1525.

## Why it matters

Frankenhausen is the moment the [[../rebellions/german-peasants-war-1524]] was effectively decided in the Thuringian north. Other peasant armies elsewhere in the empire were still in the field and were ground down over the following months, but Frankenhausen was the defining set-piece engagement and the one that fixed the rebellion in later memory.

It is also the model for how every peasant army in the early modern period died: irregular infantry against professional pike-and-shot formations backed by artillery, with the outcome essentially determined before the first arrow flew. The asymmetry is the point.
